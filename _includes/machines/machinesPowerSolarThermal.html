Solar Thermal Power Plants - STePP units

The main power source once Lalande has a genuine town, and later for the city, isn't nuclear or solar panels. It's generators running on stored solar heat. Specifically, Stirling engines using a gaseous working fluid, heated by a mass of molten regolith, which in turn is heated by tubes filled with molten sodium that circulate through parabolic mirrors.

In an earlier version, the chemicals that boil off the lava were going to be collected for use elsewhere, and the plant was sized with extra capacity so that some lava could be drawn off and used to produce things like glass and construction materials. But the plant is much more efficient and easier to build if it runs at temperatures too low for that to be viable, so now a separate solar furnace will be designed for bulk molten regolith production.

Some of the components for STePP units are simple and can be produced on-site early in colony development - mirrors, the tanks, pipes. With 3d printing of parts out of sintered metal powder and casting of molten minerals, most of the radiators, pumps, and generators can also be made. Production of efficient working units made entirely locally seems feasible sooner than the same goal for solar panels or nuclear plants, especially if early units work at lower temperatures.

We have been working on a spreadsheet that roughly calculates how well this thing would run and the power it would produce. Very roughly, and it is being added to bit by bit. It does help in thinking it through and has been a good learning tool. It will be nice to have spreadsheets, scripts, and simulations for all of these models down the road.

This shows a single mirror unit, tank complex, Stirling engine, and radiator unit, for the sake of a clear explanation. Units placed in the colonies will have as many mirror, generator, and radiator units as the tank can support, and be scaled as large as simulations indicate would work well enough. That simulating remains to be done, but guesstimating from what we have now, this concept is a good option for units producing up to 10 MW of electricity, steadily day and night.
